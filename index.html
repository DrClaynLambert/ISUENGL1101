Question 1: When I think about starting a piece of writing, my biggest barrier is...

TOC Domain: Getting Started
Textbook References: Everyone’s an Author Ch. 1 ("Thinking Like a Writer"), Lumen Unit: "Beginning the Writing Process"

Barrier TypeGoalSuggested Activities
CognitiveUnderstand what counts as "starting"—generating ideas, understanding the task.
  • Brainstorm 3 workplace tasks that involve writing (e.g., reports, instructions, notes).
  • Identify the writing purpose and audience for each task.
  • Compare a vague vs. clear writing prompt.
ApplicativeTry different ways to begin writing.
  • Create a short outline for a workplace task.
  • Freewrite about a career challenge for 5 mins.
  • Draft a workplace log or reflection paragraph.
AffectiveFeel less anxiety or uncertainty when beginning.
  • Reflect: what makes starting feel hard?
  • Build a ritual (music, walk, doodle) for easing into writing.
  • Share first sentences in a group to lower stakes.

Question 2: When I think about writing a complete draft, my biggest barrier is...

TOC Domain: Drafting
Textbook References: Everyone’s an Author Ch. 2 ("Reading and Thinking Critically"), Lumen Unit: "Drafting"

Barrier TypeGoalSuggested Activities
CognitiveUnderstand what a draft is and why it's not supposed to be perfect.
  • Compare a rough draft and final version of a workplace report.
  • Read about how professionals draft (e.g., field notes).
  • Discuss the myth of the "perfect first try."
ApplicativePractice moving from idea to full draft.
  • Write a first draft of a workplace document (e.g., instructions, observation log).
  • Try a speed-draft or a 15-minute timer challenge.
  • Draft with sentence starters: "The purpose of this report is..."
AffectiveReduce pressure and perfectionism when drafting.
  • Reflect: What stops you mid-draft?
  • Share a messy draft and celebrate the progress.
  • Use a checklist to track forward movement in writing.

Question 3: When I think about organizing my ideas or structuring my writing, my biggest barrier is...

TOC Domain: Organization & Structure
Textbook References: Everyone’s an Author Ch. 7 ("Organizing Your Ideas"), Lumen Unit: "Organizing Your Writing"

Barrier TypeGoalSuggested Activities
CognitiveUnderstand common structures in workplace and technical writing.
  • Analyze structure in a memo, set of instructions, or field report.
  • Map out common organizational patterns (chronological, problem/solution).
  • Create a structure cheat sheet by genre.
ApplicativePractice outlining and rearranging content.
  • Break a draft into sections and label them (intro, task, result).
  • Reverse-outline an existing document to understand structure.
  • Try reorganizing a paragraph or section for better flow.
AffectiveGain confidence that structure helps—not hinders—clear writing.
  • Reflect: When did structure help you understand a message?
  • Share two outlines and ask which is clearer.
  • Build a visual map or diagram before drafting.

Question 19: When I think about writing in different formats or genres, my biggest barrier is...

TOC Domain: Genre Awareness & Flexibility
Textbook References: Everyone’s an Author Ch. 4 ("Genres and Disciplines"), Lumen Unit: "Genres and Formats"

Barrier TypeGoalSuggested Activities
CognitiveUnderstand that different workplace tasks require different genres and formats.
  • Compare an email, report, checklist, and incident log for tone and purpose.
  • Identify the audience and function of each genre.
  • Create a genre guide for your field with examples.
ApplicativePractice adapting content to different genres.
  • Rewrite the same content as an email and a short report.
  • Draft a field note, then revise it for an external audience.
  • Role-play responding to a scenario using different genre forms.
AffectiveFeel empowered to choose and use genres strategically.
  • Reflect: Which genres feel most natural to you? Why?
  • Discuss a time when choosing the right format helped you communicate clearly.
  • Try a genre-swap activity with a peer.
